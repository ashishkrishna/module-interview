The Exercise

In this exercise, you'll implement some of the core math used to model the potential output of a solar panel (called a solar module by those in the industry). This represents some of the core math that goes on deep in the process of simulating a solar photovoltaic array. Our intent with this exercise is not to look for pure algorithmic skill, or to filter for people who love doing math, but simply as an interesting and relevant context to see how you structure and test your code/algorithms.

Context

To determine the electrical output of solar photovoltaic modules, the standard approach is to use a mathematical formulation based on the Shockley Diode Equation. The specific formulation for a given solar panel is given by:

\begin{equation} I=I_{sc}\cdot S - I_0(T)\left(e^{\frac{q}{kTa}\left(V+I \cdot R_s\right)}-1\right)-\frac{V+I \cdot R_s}{R_p} \end{equation}

Here, I0(T) is defining a function that causes the output of a solar panel to change slightly as temperature changes (all other things equal, at higher temperatures a module will output at about the same current, but a lower voltage). This function is given by:

\begin{equation} I_0(T)=I_0\left(1+\tau\right)^{T-25} \end{equation}

Module Output

Inputs
\(G\)
Effective Irradiance W/M2
\(T_{C}\)
Module (Cell) Temperature, K
Module Parameters
\(I_{PH}(G,T)\)
Photocurrent (e.g. Short-Circuit Current), proportional to G, (Amps)
\(I_{0}(T)\)
inverse saturation current, depending on the temperature, (Amps)
\(R_{sh}(G)\)
Shunt Resistance, varies with G, (Ohms)
\(R_{s}\)
Series Resistance, (Ohms)
\(\gamma\)
Module quality factor (typically 1-2)
\(N_{CS}\)
Number of cells in series the module
Physical Constants
\(q\)
Fundamental charge of the electron, 1.602&middot 10-19 Coulomb
\(K\)
Boltzmann constant, 1.381&middot 10-23 J/K)

Most of these coefficients are determined from published specifications, so that ultimately you can plug in variables for sunlight, S (technically this is called irradiance), and temperature, T.
